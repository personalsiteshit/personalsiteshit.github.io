"As We May Think", Vannevar Bush

Title

“As We May Think” is a text written by Vannevar Bush, and published in The Atlantic in 1945. Bush was an American Engineer, scientist and inventor who took a central part in the development of the atomic bomb and overseer of the Manhattan project (Atomic Heritage Foundation 1). Written at the end of the second World War, Bush comments that the central focus of physics based work was weaponry and combat mobilization, and further asks where now physicists will find work that will be “worthy of their best" (Bush 1).

Bush explains that the current practices in finding scholarly work is well outdated considering the increasing number of scientific publications, using the example of Mendel, stating “Mendel's concept of the laws of genetics was lost to the world for a generation because his publication did not reach the few who were capable of grasping and extending it;” (Bush 3). He furthers this thought by reflecting on the antiquity of technology, and the rapid course of the evolution of machinery that is taking place or will be taking place in the near future.

Bush now approaches his thesis, stating “The real heart of the matter of selection, however, goes deeper than a lag in the adoption of mechanisms by libraries, or a lack of development of devices for their use. Our ineptitude in getting at the record is largely caused by the artificiality of systems of indexing. (Bush 15)” He addresses the issue of the intuitiveness of modern indexing, explaining that the human mind does not operate by the logic of indexing. In this sense, if the human mind operates by association, finding scholarly articles, which are growing exponentially, will be impossible with mechanisms that are unintuitive to the human mind. Through this conclusion, Bush proposes a future invention coined the memex. The memex is Bush’s solution to unintuitive indexing, further elaborating “If affords an immediate step, however, to associative indexing, the basic idea of which is a provision whereby any item may be caused at will to select immediately and automatically another. This is the essential feature of the memex. The process of tying two items together is the important thing. (Bush, 16)”

The logic Bush proposed lay inspiration for modern inventors, and is the framework by which the web operates. More astounding is his insight when taken into consideration the publication date of the article. To the credit of Bush, the web today has drastically decreased the time and effort spent finding related work, and has been essential in modern day scientific publications.

"From Counterculture to Cyberculture", Fred Turner

Title

From Counterculture to Cyberculture is a text written by Fred Turner. It recounts the history of the internet and personal computing, starting from pre WWII up until the early 2000’s. The text itself largely follows paragons of personal computing, and each of their respective impacts in its domain. It elaborates on the evolution of computers, in size and computing power, as well as the internet.

The main idea in this text is the crossover by peers and professionals in the industry of each scientist documented. It illustrates how various individual projects all tie together in the central goal of Counterculture; counterculture being the autonomy that is the personal computer. It is a device which allows individuals the freedom of information: where computers were once gargantuan machines kept in basements of universities, scrupulously watched and protected, the innovations of cybernetics now allow for a smaller (or portable) computer for personal and private use.

Furthermore, Turner illustrates how the function of computing drastically changed: which was once a machine purely for calculation has now become an intrinsic element in human social interaction; a device which revolutionized communication through the various projects that helped bring the internet to fruition.

This was spurred by the worry by many of American capitalism, which if left unchecked, would “eat Canada’s lunch”.
